挨拶【あい・さつ】

Basic Japanese Greetings

It is always good to learn some basic Japanese greetings at the start of learning this new language. You may not understand the real meaning of each word at this moment. Just give your best shot at memorizing some of them, or all of them, if you can. In time, you will get to the stage of understanding the meanings of all the words. At the same time, this may be your first time speaking in Japanese. It may sound weird. But it's fun. Try it out. Everyone has his or her first time.

Meeting Japanese Friend For The First Time

When meeting a Japanese friend for the first time, the greetings that we will normally say are:

はじめまして。
How do you do? (This sentence is used only if you are meeting this person for the first time)

わたしはジョンです。
My name is John. (Assuming your name is John)

どうぞよろしくおねがいします。
I am pleased to meet you. (Literally the meaning for this sentence is "Please form good relations with me" or "Please take good care of me")

And most likely, your Japanese friend will reply you with:

こちらこそ、どうぞよろしくおねがいします。
The pleasure is all mine.
I am pleased to meet you. (Literally the meaning for this sentence is "Please form good relations with me" or "Please take good care of me")

わたしはたなかです。
My name is Tanaka. (Assuming your Japanese friend's name is Tanaka)

名詞【めい・し】

Declaring something is so and so using 「だ」

Vocabulary

1. あなた - you
2. 私【わたし】- I
3. 人【ひと】- person
4. 学生【がく・せい】- student
5. 会社員【かい・しゃ・いん】- office worker
6. 勉強【べんきょう】- study
7. する - do

One of the trickiest part of Japanese is that there is no verb for the state-of-being like the verb "to be" in English. You can, however, declare what something is by attaching the Hiragana character 「だ」 to a noun or 形容動詞【けい・よう・どう・し】 only. (We will learn about 形容動詞【けい・よう・どう・し】 in the section on adjectives.)

Examples

1. 人だ。
Is person.

2. 学生だ。
Is student.

3. 会社員だ
Is office worker

Seems easy enough. Here's the real kicker though.

A state-of-being can be implied without using . 「だ」!

You can say you're doing well or someone is a student without using 「だ」 at all. For example, below is an example of a very typical greeting among friends. Also notice how the subject isn't even specified when it's obvious from the context.

Typical casual greeting

A:あなたは会社員?
A: Are you well an office worker?

B:うん。会社員。
B: Yes, (I'm) an office worker.

So you may be wondering, "What's the point of using 「だ」?" Well, the main difference is that a declarative statement makes the sentence sound more emphatic and forceful in order to make it more... well declarative. Therefore, it is more common to hear men use 「だ」 at the end of sentences.

The declarative 「だ」 is also needed in various grammatical structures where a state-of-being must be explicitly declared. There are also times when you cannot attach it. It's all quite a pain in the butt really but you don't have to worry about it yet.

Conjugating to the negative state-of-being

In Japanese, negative and past tense are all expressed by conjugation. We can conjugate a noun or adjective to either its negative or past tense to say that something is not [X] or that something was [X]. This may be a bit hard to grasp at first but none of these state-of-being conjugations make anything declarative like 「だ」 does.

Conjugation rules for 名詞【めい・し】

人 だ Base
ひと ではない Negative
ひと だった Past-tense
ひと で Connective
ひと だったら Predictive I
ひと なら Predictive II

Example

1. 私は会社員だ。
I am an office worker.

2. 私は学生ではない。
I am not a student.

4. 私は学生だった。
I was a student.

3. 私は学生で会社員だ。
I am a student and an office worker.

4. 私は学生だったら、勉強をする。
If I am a student, I will study.

5. あなたは会社員なら、私は学生だ。
If you are an office worker, I am a student.

Demonstrative Pronouns - こ・そ・あ・ど Series

There is an obvious distinction between demonstrative pronouns and demonstrative adjectives in Japanese. 'This' and 'that' in the Japanese language are both examples of the former as well as the latter. You might have noticed that native Japanese used these three words: これ・それ・あれ to refer to things or people around them.

These three are the most commonly used demonstrative pronouns in Japanese. Unlike in the English language where 'this + bag' or 'that + bag' are followed by nouns, demonstrative pronouns in Japanese are not followed by a noun.

There are three demonstrative adjectives in Japanese: この・その・あの.

Demonstrative adjectives in the Japanese language are followed by a noun, similar to English i.e. this bag, that bag etc.

これ・それ・あれ

The three most commonly used Japanese demonstrative pronouns are これ・それ・あれ. While in English we identify objects by "this" and "that", Japanese natives identify objects by "this", "that", "that over there".

これ refers to an object near to the speaker
それ explains an object near to the listener
あれ dictates an object far from both the speaker and the listener

Everything is in reference from the point of view of the speaker. So the speaker can point to a bag in front of him and say:

これはわたしのかばんです。
This is my bag.

The speaker can point to a bag in front of the listener and say:

それはあなたのかばんです。
That is your bag.

The speaker can then point to a bag far away from both of them and say:

あれはジョンさんのかばんです。
That (over there) is John's bag.

Japanese demonstrative pronouns

Depending on the situation, sometimes if both the speaker and the listener are close enough, they can consider themselves as one entity. Therefore they can refer to object near to them as これ, refer to object slightly away from them as それ and object far away from them as あれ.


There is an associated question word どれ for these demonstrative pronouns. You can use this to ask question when you want to determine certain object from within a group of similar objects.

あなたのかばんはどれですか。
Which (one of these bags) is you bag?

この・その・あの

These three Japanese demonstrative adjectives modify nouns that follow after them. So the formats are always この + Noun, その + Noun and あの + Noun. They are used to identify objects in a similar way.

この + Noun refers to an object or person near to the speaker
その + Noun refers to an object or person near to the listener
あの + Noun refers to an object or person far from both the speaker and the listener

Using the same example, the speaker will point to a bag in front of him and say:

このかばんはわたしのです。
This bag is mine.

The speaker will point to a bag in front of the listener and say:

そのかばんはあなたのです。
That bag is yours.

The speaker can then point to a bag far away from both of them and say:

あのかばんはジョンさんのです。
That bag over there is John's.

Japanese demonstrative adjectives

Notice that when the same noun is repeated in a sentence, it can be omitted, just like the かばん in the above examples.

When both the speaker and the listener are close enough, they can consider themselves as one entity and refer to an object/person near to them as この + Noun . They would also refer to an object/person slightly away from them as その + Noun and an object/person far away from them as あの + Noun.

Japanese demonstrative adjectives

And the associated question word for these demonstrative adjectives is どの. You can use this to ask question to determine certain object/person from within a group:

わたしのかばんはどのかばんですか。
Which one is my bag? / Which one of (these bags) is mine?

ここ・そこ・あそこ

While これ・それ・あれ refer to things, another three demonstrative pronouns ここ・そこ・あそこ refer to places.

Japanese demonstrative pronouns

ここ refers to the place where the speaker is
そこ points to the place where the listener is
あそこ indicates the place which is far from both the speaker and the listener

These three not only refer to places; they can also be used to point someone in a particular direction. The associated question word is どちら.
